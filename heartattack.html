Heart Attack

Stages of a Heart Attack

Here you can learn about the stages of a heart attack.

Stages of a heart attack:

    1. A coronary blood vessel is blocked, leading to inadequate blood supply (ischaemia) and subsequently mass cell death (necrosis)
  1. 2. If the damage is not extensive enough to be life-threatening, your body’s repair mechanisms will kick in and initiate an immune response, with neutrophils clearing the dead tissue and fibroblasts forming clots to seal the wound
  2. 3. Over a few months, your heart will continue to repair, but the cardiomyocytes in your myocardium (the contractile muscle cells in your heart) will never regenerate. In its place, fibroblasts will replace the blood clot with collagen deposits. Although collagen is a tough material suitable for closing wounds, it can never replace the cardiomyocytes’ contractility and thus reduce the ability of the heart to pump blood around the body
  3. 4. This makes you more vulnerable to additional infarction events and compensatory pathology such as arrhythmias, myocardial ruptures and ventricular aneurysms.
Unfortunately, there is currently no cure to these post-infarct events. Heart transplantations are rare, and cardiac regeneration is still not a reality, so the only solution is to maintain a healthy lifestyle. This makes it all the more important to follow a proper diet and exercise regime!
What is a heart attack caused by?
A heart attack can have numerous causes.
The most common of these are: atherosclerosis, aneurysm and thrombosis.

Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis is the formation of cholesterol plaques against the blood vessel walls. Although this is a natural process with ageing, the speed of onset and pathology can be altered by lifestyle factors. Atherosclerosis is a manageable disease but drastically increases the risk for life-threatening heart conditions. Click here for the main types of heart disease.

A blood vessel is made of three main layers:

  • Tunica intima
    This is the inner most layer of a blood vessel and contains one layer of endothelial cells, a small amount of endothelial space and the internal elastic lamina which gives the blood vessel its elastic property
    The endothelial cells also release nitric oxide (NO) which regulates the contraction and relaxation necessary for shuttling the blood along the body
  • Tunica media
    Contains the vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs), which contract and relax. It is regulated by the NO released by the endothelial cells.
    Also has external elastic lamina
  • Tunica adventitia
    A layer of connective tissue which protects the vascular wall from external shock.
    Note that the width and presence of these layers alters in accordance to the type (artery/vein) and size (artery/arteriole/capillary) + (large vein/medium vein/venule).
    The space inside the blood vessel wall is known as the lumen.

    • Stages of Atherogenesis:

    PRE-LESION:

    1. 1. Endothelial Injury: the underlying endothelium allows lipoproteins (which carry fat such as and cholesterol around the body) to migrate to the sub-endothelial space, resulting in a ‘fatty streak’ as fat accumulates in the blood vessel walls and undergoes oxidation which damages the blood vessel wall
    2. 2. Immune response: your immune system kicks in and monocytes adhere to the blood vessel wall. They migrate to the sub-endothelial space where they mature into macrophages (white blood cells) which begin to engulf the accumulated fat.
    3. 3. Immune response (continued): chemical signals are released and T cells (your body’s killer cells) invade the intima. VSMCs also become dysregulated and migrate from the media to the intima, worsening the condition
      At this point, high blood pressure becomes a consequential condition due to a reduced lumen diameter, reducing flow and increasing resistance as blood is being squeezed through the blood vessel.

      LESION:

    4. 4. After migration to the intimal layer, VSMCs proliferate. Lipids continue to accumulate and oxidise. Macrophages become ‘foamy’ as they engulf lots of fat

      ADVANCED LESION:

    5. 5. The continued build-up of VSMCs, fat and macrophages results in the formation of a necrotic core made of dead cells such as macrophages and their fatty contents. A fibrous cap is formed by a new layer of VSMCs lining the outside of the necrotic core to form a neointima. An eccentric lesion is formed, resulting in a ‘ballooning effect’ which stretches the vessel. An advanced lesion can further progress to an aneurysm or lead to thrombosis (Witting, 2015).

    Further Complications:

    Aneurysm
    Aneurysm occurs if the lesion breaks, causing a blockage of the vessel as all the contents of the necrotic core spill into the blood vessel. If this aneurysm occurs in a coronary blood vessel, it will cause sudden cardiac death.

    Thrombosis
    Thrombosis occurs if the lesion increases to a point where it blocks the vessel, it will cause thrombosis. It also has the potential to detach, becoming an embolic thrombus which is more dangerous as it has the potential to travel to an important blood vessel e.g. coronary blood vessel or a capillary supplying an important area in the body, leading to death or severe heart conditions(Hambly, 2015).
